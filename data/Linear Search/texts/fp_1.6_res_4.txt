Preschool education is a topic of much debate and discussion among educators, parents, and policymakers. While some argue that attending preschool can have significant benefits for children's academic success later on, others question whether it really makes a difference. In this essay, I will explore the research surrounding the question: do children who attend preschool do better in school?

There is a substantial body of evidence to suggest that attending preschool can have positive impacts on children's educational outcomes. Numerous studies have shown that children who attend high-quality preschool programs tend to perform better academically in elementary school compared to those who did not attend preschool. One study conducted by the National Institute for Early Education Research found that students who attended high-quality pre-K programs had higher math and reading scores in kindergarten than their peers who did not attend such programs.

Additionally, research has shown that students who attend preschool are more likely to graduate from high school and pursue higher education than those who do not receive early childhood education. A longitudinal study published by the Journal of Educational Psychology found that students who attended pre-K were more likely to graduate from high school and enroll in college compared to their peers.

Preschool also provides important socialization opportunities for young children, helping them develop crucial social skills such as cooperation, communication, empathy, and problem-solving abilities. These skills are essential for success both inside and outside of the classroom.

Moreover, early childhood education has been shown to narrow achievement gaps between low-income students and their wealthier counterparts. Research has consistently demonstrated that economically disadvantaged children benefit greatly from access to quality pre-K programs which can help level the playing field when they enter primary school.
