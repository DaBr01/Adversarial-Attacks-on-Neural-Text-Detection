Research has shown that exposure to language, literacy, math, and social skills at a young age can help children develop the cognitive and emotional skills needed to succeed in school. Preschool can also help children learn how to follow routines and take direction from teachers, which are important aspects of success in a classroom setting.

Furthermore, children who attend preschool may have an edge when they enter kindergarten because they are more likely to be familiar with basic concepts like shapes, colors, numbers, and letters. This early exposure can give them a head start compared to children who did not attend preschool. In addition, studies have shown that attendance at high-quality preschool programs can lead to higher rates of graduation from high school and even college attendance.

On the other hand, critics of preschool education argue that any short-term gains in academic performance are unlikely to be sustained in the long run. They point out that the effects of preschool often fade away by third grade or middle school, suggesting that the benefits are not as significant as proponents claim. Additionally, some argue that preschool programs can put too much emphasis on structured activities and academic skills at the expense of play and social interaction, which are also important aspects of early childhood development.

Another concern raised by skeptics is the potential for preschool education to exacerbate existing achievement gaps between low-income families and their wealthier counterparts. If only certain groups of children have access to high-quality preschool programs, then attending preschool could actually widen disparities in academic achievement rather than narrow them.

Overall, there is mixed evidence on whether children who attend preschool do better in school. While some studies suggest that early childhood education can have positive impacts on academic performance, others question the long-term sustainability of these effects and highlight potential drawbacks of focusing too much on structured learning at a young age.

In conclusion, while attending preschool may provide some advantages for children in terms of their readiness for formal schooling, it is not a guarantee of success in later years. Ultimately, the quality of the program and how it complements a child's individual needs and abilities will determine its effectiveness. It is important for parents and educators to consider all factors before deciding whether preschool is right for a particular child.
