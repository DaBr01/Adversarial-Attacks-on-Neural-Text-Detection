As the world continues to progress towards inclusivity and equal opportunities for all individuals, the question of whether universities should provide accommodations to disabled students becomes increasingly important. It is essential for educational institutions to ensure that all students, regardless of their physical or mental abilities, have access to the same quality of education and opportunities for success.

One argument in favor of universities providing accommodations to disabled students is that it ensures equal access to educational opportunities. Without appropriate accommodations, many students with disabilities may struggle to participate fully in classes, complete assignments, or take exams. Providing accommodations such as extended time on exams, note-taking services, assistive technology, or accessible classrooms can level the playing field for these students and allow them to succeed academically.

In addition to ensuring equal access to education, providing accommodations to disabled students can also benefit the university as a whole. By creating an inclusive environment where all students feel supported and able to succeed, universities can attract a diverse student body and foster a sense of community among students. This can lead to improved retention rates, higher graduation rates, and increased diversity among graduates, which can ultimately enhance the reputation and standing of the university.

Furthermore, providing accommodations to disabled students aligns with legal requirements and obligations under disability rights legislation. The Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA) mandates that schools and universities must provide reasonable accommodations to students with disabilities in order to ensure equal access to education. Failure to do so not only puts universities at risk of legal action but also goes against the principles of equality and inclusion that society strives to uphold.

On the other hand, some may argue that accommodating disabled students places undue burden on universities in terms of time, resources, and costs. However, it is important to recognize that providing accommodations is not only a legal requirement but also a moral imperative. Denying disabled students access to necessary accommodations deprives them of their right to education and perpetuates barriers that prevent them from reaching their full potential.
