Universities are institutions that aim to educate students and provide them with the necessary tools to succeed in their academic and professional careers. In recent years, there has been a growing recognition of the importance of providing accommodations for disabled students in higher education institutions. The question of whether universities should provide accommodations to disabled students is a complex issue that involves considerations of fairness, equality, and inclusivity.

One of the main arguments in favor of providing accommodations to disabled students is that it promotes equal access to education. Under the Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA), universities are required to provide reasonable accommodations to students with disabilities in order to ensure that they have access to the same educational opportunities as their non-disabled peers. Accommodations can include things such as extended time on exams, note-taking services, and assistive technology.

By providing accommodations to disabled students, universities can help level the playing field and give these students an equal opportunity to succeed academically. Without appropriate accommodations, disabled students may struggle to keep up with their coursework and may be at a disadvantage compared to their non-disabled peers. Providing accommodations can help address these disparities and create a more inclusive learning environment for all students.

Another argument in favor of providing accommodations is that it promotes diversity and inclusivity on campus. By accommodating the needs of disabled students, universities can create a more welcoming and supportive environment for all members of the campus community. This can help foster a sense of belonging among disabled students and can contribute to a more diverse and inclusive student body.

Furthermore, providing accommodations can have positive benefits for all students, not just those with disabilities. For example, accessible classrooms and materials can benefit all learners by making information easier to access and understand. However, there are also arguments against providing accommodations to disabled students. Some critics may argue that accommodating the needs of disabled students is too costly or burdensome for universities. They may also argue that providing accommodations could be seen as giving preferential treatment to certain groups of students.

Ultimately, the question of whether universities should provide accommodations to disabled students is a complex issue that involves balancing competing interests and considerations. However, the evidence suggests that providing accommodations is not only required by law but also has important benefits for both disabled and non-disabled students.
