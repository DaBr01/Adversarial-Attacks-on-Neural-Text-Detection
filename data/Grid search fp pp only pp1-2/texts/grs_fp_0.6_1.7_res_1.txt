In recent years, the importance of accessibility and inclusion for disabled individuals has gained significant attention. As a result, many universities have been making efforts to ensure that their campuses are accessible to all students, including those with disabilities. One key aspect of this is providing accommodations to disabled students in order to help them succeed academically.

There are several reasons why universities should provide accommodations to disabled students. Firstly, it is important to note that disability does not equate to inability. Many disabled individuals are fully capable of excelling in an academic environment if given the appropriate support. By providing accommodations such as assistive technology, note-taking services, extended time on exams, or sign language interpreters, universities can level the playing field for disabled students and ensure that they have equal access to education.

Secondly, providing accommodations to disabled students is not only a matter of fairness but also a legal requirement. Under the Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA) and Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act of 1973, universities are obligated to provide reasonable accommodations to qualified students with disabilities in order to ensure equal opportunity and non-discrimination. Failure to do so could result in legal consequences for the university.

Additionally, providing accommodations for disabled students benefits not only the individual student but also the university community as a whole. When diverse perspectives and experiences are valued and included, it enriches the learning environment for everyone. Disabled students bring unique insights and contributions to campus life, and by accommodating their needs, universities demonstrate their commitment to diversity and inclusivity.

Furthermore, research has shown that when students receive adequate support and accommodations for their disabilities, they are more likely to persist in their studies and achieve academic success. For example, a study published in The Journal of Higher Education found that disabled college students who received appropriate accommodations were more likely than their peers without disabilities to graduate within six years.

On the other hand, some may argue that providing accommodations for disabled students places an undue burden on universities in terms of cost and resources.
