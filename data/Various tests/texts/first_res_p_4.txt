In the real world, there is a growing body of research that supports the idea that children who attend preschool do better in school.

One of the main reasons why children who attend preschool tend to do better in school is that preschool provides a strong foundation for future academic success. Preschool helps children develop critical skills such as early literacy and numeracy, problem-solving, and social skills. These skills are essential for success in school and in life, and children who attend preschool are better equipped to handle the academic challenges that they will face in later years.

In addition to providing a strong academic foundation, preschool also helps children develop important social and emotional skills. In preschool, children learn how to interact with their peers, resolve conflicts, and regulate their emotions. These skills are crucial for success in school and in social situations, and children who attend preschool are more likely to have the social and emotional skills needed to thrive in the classroom.

Furthermore, research has shown that children who attend preschool are more likely to graduate from high school and attend college. A study conducted by the National Institute for Early Education Research found that children who attended preschool were more likely to graduate from high school and attend college than children who did not attend preschool. This is likely due to the fact that preschool helps children develop the skills and habits needed to succeed academically, setting them on a path towards future success.

On a personal level, I have seen the benefits of preschool in my own children. My oldest child attended preschool and has excelled in school ever since. She is a confident and outgoing student who enjoys learning and interacting with her peers. I credit much of her success to the strong foundation that she received in preschool. My youngest child is currently attending preschool, and I can already see the positive impact it is having on her development. She is learning new skills, making friends, and gaining confidence in herself.

In conclusion, the evidence suggests that children who attend preschool do better in school. Preschool provides a strong foundation for academic success, helps children develop important social and emotional skills, and sets them on a path towards future success. My own experiences as a parent support this idea, as I have seen the positive impact that preschool has had on my own children. As a result, I believe that sending children to preschool is a wise investment in their future.
