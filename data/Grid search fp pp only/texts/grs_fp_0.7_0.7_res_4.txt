Many parents believe that attending preschool can provide their child with a strong academic foundation and better prepare them for success in school. However, the question of whether children who attend preschool actually perform better academically than those who do not is still a topic of debate among educators and researchers.

Research studies have shown varying results when it comes to the impact of preschool on academic performance. Some studies indicate that children who attend preschool show higher levels of school readiness and are more likely to succeed academically compared to those who do not attend. For example, a study conducted by the National Institute for Early Education Research found that children who attended high-quality preschool programs showed significant gains in language, literacy, and math skills compared to their peers who did not attend preschool.

Furthermore, research has also shown that children enrolled in high-quality preschool programs are less likely to require special education services later on in school. A study published in the Journal of Economic Perspectives found that students who had access to early childhood education were significantly less likely to be placed in special education classes or be held back a grade.

Moreover, research suggests that attending preschool can have long-term benefits for children's academic success. A study published in the journal Child Development followed children from low-income families over several years and found that those who attended a quality prekindergarten program were more likely to graduate from high school and continue on to college compared to those who did not attend preschool.
